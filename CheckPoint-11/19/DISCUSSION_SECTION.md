## Summary of Results and Conclusions

The results of this study demonstrate that the packeted-aggregate training approach produced substantial improvements in depression classification performance when evaluated on aggregated user-level test data, but minimal differences when evaluated on individual messages. When tested on 3,419 aggregated user packets, the fine-tuned model achieved an accuracy of 73.97%, representing a 14.19 percentage point improvement over the default DepRoBERTa baseline (59.78%). This improvement exceeded the predicted 0.05 accuracy gain, with the fine-tuned model showing a 23.7% relative improvement in accuracy and a 48.2% relative improvement in F1 score (73.08% vs. 49.32%). The model's precision and recall were both balanced at approximately 74%, indicating consistent performance across both classes. However, when evaluated on 10,000 individual messages extracted from the same users, the fine-tuned model showed virtually identical accuracy to the default baseline (42.01% vs. 42.27%), with only a modest improvement in F1 score (36.36% vs. 25.90%). This pattern suggests that the packeted-aggregate structure enables the model to capture temporal linguistic patterns that are distributed across multiple messages, but these patterns are not effectively preserved when the model is applied to isolated individual messages that lack temporal context.

The observed performance improvements on aggregated data can be explained through the preservation of longitudinal linguistic signals that characterize depressive expression. Research by König et al. (2022) has demonstrated that depressive language manifests as sustained patterns across time rather than isolated markers within single posts, including reduced lexical diversity, consistent negative sentiment trajectories, and evolving patterns of self-reference. When messages are aggregated into temporally ordered packets, the model can learn relationships between sequential posts that indicate gradual emotional progression—a characteristic clinical sign of depression that single-message approaches cannot capture. The substantial reduction in false positives (from 1,283 to 628) when comparing the fine-tuned model to the default baseline on aggregated data further supports this interpretation, as the model learned to distinguish between transient negative expressions and sustained depressive patterns. Conversely, the lack of improvement on individual messages indicates that the fine-tuned model's learned patterns are specifically contextual to the aggregated structure, suggesting that temporal continuity plays a critical role in the model's effectiveness (Bucor et al., 2023).

Statistical analysis of the results indicates that the performance differences between models are highly significant, particularly for aggregated evaluation. The fine-tuned model's accuracy of 73.97% on aggregated data falls well outside the standard error range of the baseline model (59.78% ± 0.84%), with the improvement of 14.19 percentage points representing approximately 17 standard errors—far exceeding the threshold for statistical significance. The standard error bars calculated using the formula √(p(1-p)/n) for the fine-tuned model's accuracy (0.0075) and the baseline model's accuracy (0.0084) demonstrate non-overlapping confidence intervals, confirming that the improvement is statistically meaningful rather than due to sampling variability. Similarly, the F1 score improvement of 23.76 percentage points (from 49.32% to 73.08%) represents a substantial effect size that cannot be attributed to chance variation. However, the performance on individual messages showed no statistically significant difference in accuracy (42.01% vs. 42.27%, difference of 0.26 percentage points), with overlapping standard error ranges, indicating that the fine-tuning approach did not meaningfully improve single-message classification performance. These statistical patterns support the conclusion that data organization through packet aggregation is the primary factor driving improved performance, rather than general model fine-tuning alone.

The results partially support the research hypothesis that training a transformer-based NLP model on packeted, user-level groupings of messages would result in higher depression classification accuracy than training on individual messages, with important qualifications regarding evaluation context. The hypothesis predicted that the packeted-aggregate approach would outperform the baseline DepRoBERTa model by at least 0.05 in classification accuracy for both aggregate-packets and individual messages, with an expected improvement from approximately 0.658 to 0.708. The results strongly support the hypothesis when evaluated on aggregated test data, as the fine-tuned model achieved 73.97% accuracy—substantially exceeding both the predicted 70.8% and the baseline 59.78%—representing an improvement of 14.19 percentage points, far exceeding the predicted minimum of 5 percentage points. However, the hypothesis is not supported when evaluated on individual messages, where the fine-tuned model showed no meaningful accuracy improvement (42.01% vs. 42.27%). This partial support suggests that the packeted-aggregate training structure enables the model to learn contextual patterns that are effective when applied to aggregated data, but these learned patterns are not transferable to single-message classification tasks, indicating that the evaluation context must match the training structure for optimal performance. The disparity between aggregated and individual message performance aligns with the theoretical framework proposed by König et al. (2022), which emphasizes that depressive linguistic signals are distributed temporally rather than concentrated in isolated statements.

The conclusions drawn from these results indicate that data organization through user-level packet aggregation is a critical factor in improving depression classification performance, independent of model architecture modifications. The substantial performance gains achieved on aggregated data (14.19% accuracy improvement) demonstrate that restructuring training data to preserve temporal continuity enables transformer models to capture longitudinal linguistic patterns that single-message training approaches miss. This finding is consistent with established principles in clinical psychology, where depression diagnosis relies on evaluating behavioral trends over time rather than isolated statements, as emphasized by König et al. (2022). The pattern observed—where aggregation improves aggregated evaluation but not individual message evaluation—suggests that the learned patterns are context-dependent and require the same structural organization at both training and inference time. However, the lack of improvement on individual messages indicates a limitation: while the packeted approach is effective for user-level screening, it may not improve real-time classification of individual social media posts as they are generated. The balanced precision and recall (both ~74%) achieved by the fine-tuned model on aggregated data is particularly important for mental health applications, as it indicates the model maintains sensitivity to depression cases while avoiding excessive false positives, which is crucial for clinical decision support systems where both false negatives and false positives carry significant consequences.

Several variables beyond experimental control may have influenced the results, including the inherent characteristics of the Reddit Mental Health Dataset and platform-specific linguistic conventions. The dataset was collected from specific mental health subreddits with established community norms and discussion patterns that may not generalize to other social media platforms or communication contexts. Additionally, the aggregation process grouped messages by user rather than by temporal proximity, which means that messages spanning long time periods were combined, potentially diluting immediate temporal relationships that might be more informative for depression detection. The class imbalance in the aggregated test set (59.3% depression vs. 40.7% non-depression) and the different imbalance in the individual message test set (42.3% depression vs. 57.7% non-depression) may have contributed to the observed performance differences, as classification algorithms can be sensitive to class distribution shifts between training and evaluation contexts. The variation in message length and content type (posts vs. comments) within the aggregated packets, along with potential confounds from subreddit-specific language conventions, represent additional uncontrolled variables that could have influenced model performance.

## Future Studies and Research Impact

A critical question that emerges from these results is whether hybrid approaches that combine temporal aggregation with individual message analysis could further improve depression detection accuracy while maintaining real-time classification capabilities. This research question is motivated by the observation that the packeted-aggregate approach excels on aggregated evaluation but shows no improvement on individual messages, suggesting that a model capable of leveraging both temporal context and immediate message-level signals might achieve superior performance across both evaluation contexts. To test this question, a dual-stream transformer architecture could be implemented, with one stream processing individual messages and a second stream processing temporally aggregated user histories, followed by a fusion layer that combines representations from both streams. This architecture would be trained on a dataset similar to the current study but evaluated both on aggregated packets and individual messages, allowing direct comparison of whether incorporating both granular and aggregated perspectives improves performance relative to single-stream approaches. The evaluation would include metrics for both real-time classification (individual messages) and user-level screening (aggregated packets), assessing whether the hybrid approach can maintain the performance gains observed in aggregated evaluation while also improving individual message classification. This proposed study is supported by the current findings, which demonstrate that temporal context significantly enhances aggregated performance, as well as by recent work on multimodal transformer architectures that suggests combining multiple information streams can improve mental health classification (Bucor et al., 2023).

The findings from this study contribute to the broader body of knowledge in computational mental health and natural language processing by demonstrating that data organization strategies can produce substantial performance improvements independent of architectural innovations. While much recent research in NLP for mental health has focused on developing novel model architectures or expanding training data volume, these results indicate that carefully structuring training data to match clinical diagnostic practices—evaluating patterns over time rather than isolated statements—can yield comparable or greater performance gains with existing model architectures. This insight has practical implications for deploying depression detection systems in clinical and research settings, as it suggests that user-level aggregation may be a necessary preprocessing step for achieving clinically useful performance levels. The study also highlights an important limitation for real-time applications: if individual messages must be classified immediately as they are generated, current aggregation-based approaches may not provide improvements over baseline models. However, for applications where user-level screening can be performed periodically or retrospectively—such as clinical risk assessment, research population screening, or community-level mental health monitoring—the packeted-aggregate approach offers a promising path toward more accurate and clinically aligned depression detection systems. These findings add to the growing evidence that temporal context is crucial for mental health NLP applications, supporting the broader movement toward time-enriched and longitudinal approaches in computational psychiatry (Bucor et al., 2023; König et al., 2022).
